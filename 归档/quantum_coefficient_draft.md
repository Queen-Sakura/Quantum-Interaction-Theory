## 2. Introduction

Quantum mechanics was formulated in 1925–1927. Classical mechanics was formulated in 1687. For nearly a century, the relationship between them has been described as "complementary," "emergent," or "contradictory." The Copenhagen interpretation axiomatically separated the quantum system from the classical observer. Decoherence theory (Zurek, 1991) moved the boundary to the environment but preserved the presupposition of a classical domain. Relational quantum mechanics (Rovelli, 1996) eliminated the privileged observer but did not provide a quantitative mechanism for the emergence of classicality.

We propose a unified quantitative framework in which classicality is not a separate domain but a limiting case of quantum physics under high-frequency interactional mutual locking. The framework introduces a single dimensionless parameter Q ∈ [−1, 1], computable from the physical properties of any system, that locates the system on the quantum-classical spectrum.

## 2. The Quantum Zeno Effect as Interactional Mutual Locking

The standard quantum Zeno effect describes the freezing of a quantum system's evolution under frequent projective measurements. The survival probability after N measurements at intervals τ = T/N is:

P_survive ≈ 1 − (Ωτ)²N ≈ 1 − Ω²Tτ

As τ → 0 (measurement frequency → ∞), survival → 1: the system is frozen in its initial state.

We reinterpret "measurement" as **interaction** — mutual quantum interaction between particles. No external observer is required. When particle A interacts with particle B (via any fundamental force), A "reads" B's quantum state and B simultaneously "reads" A's. Each interaction projects both particles into a correlated subspace. At high interaction frequency, the Zeno effect locks both particles into a shared classical configuration.

This is **interactional mutual locking** (formerly "Zeno mutual locking"). It is the engine of classical emergence.

## 3. The Quantum Coefficient Q

Define the locking probability:

P_lock = 1 − exp(−α · ν² · N · f²)

where:
- ν: **interaction frequency** (Hz) — the rate of mutual interaction between particles
- N: **network size** — the number of particles in the mutually interacting ensemble
- f² = |⟨ψᵢ|ψⱼ⟩|²: **interaction fidelity** — the overlap between the quantum states of interacting particles
- α: **coupling constant** — system-dependent normalization

The quantum coefficient is then:

**Q = 1 − 2 · P_lock**

Q ∈ [−1, 1] with three distinguished regimes:

| Q | Regime | Physics |
|---|--------|---------|
| Q = +1 | Pure quantum | Zero interactions. Isolated single particles, cosmic inflation-era universe |
| Q → 0 | Mixed / Statistical | Partial locking. Decoherence regime, classical probability |
| Q = −1 | Zeno-Newton | Complete locking. Macroscopic classical objects, Laplacian determinism |

## 5. Theoretical Implications

### 5.1 Classicality is not a separate domain

Newtonian mechanics is the Q → −1 limit of quantum mechanics under infinite interactional locking. No additional postulate is required. The three-century division between "quantum" and "classical" physics is a semantic artifact of not having the Q parameter.

### 5.2 The environment does not cause classicality; classicality is the environment

Decoherence theory treats the environment as an external classical reservoir. In our framework, the environment itself is a mutually locked quantum network. What we call "environment" is the set of particles with Q ≈ −1. The classical background is not presupposed — it is explained.

### 5.3 Q is a continuous observable

Just as temperature T quantifies "how thermal" a system is, Q quantifies "how classical" a system is. Both are emergent, continuous, and computable from microscopic parameters.

## 6. Decoherence (No Such Thing) as Routing

Decoherence theory posits that quantum coherence is *destroyed* by interaction with a classical environment. In the interactional mutual locking framework, coherence is never destroyed — it is *routed*.

When a quantum system appears to decohere, what has actually occurred is entanglement swapping through the interactional network. The quantum information that was localized at one node has been transferred to a distant node — without traversing the intermediate classical channel. The apparent "loss" of coherence at the local measurement port is a routing event, not a deletion event.

**Conservation of total entanglement information.** In a closed interactional network, total entanglement is conserved. Local Q decays not because coherence is "destroyed by environment," but because entanglement has been routed to nodes outside the observer's measurement horizon. The environment is not a classical bath — it is a quantum router operating on the same interactional principles as the observed system.

**Experimental paradigm.** Previous experiments attempt to *exclude* the environment (Faraday cages, cryogenic isolation). Our proposed experiment *embraces* the environment. We do not control the routing path — that is handled by the underlying interactional network. We only align the sender and receiver, analogous to SSH key exchange over an untrusted network. If entanglement correlations are observed between endpoints without corresponding classical signal propagation, the routing hypothesis is confirmed.

**Prediction.** Routing efficiency η = 1 − P_lock. At Q → +1 (low interaction frequency, low N), η → 1: entanglement passes cleanly. As Q → −1 (high locking), η → 0: the network is too frozen to route. There exists a critical Q_c at which routing suddenly fails — a phase transition in the interactional network, not a gradual decoherence.
