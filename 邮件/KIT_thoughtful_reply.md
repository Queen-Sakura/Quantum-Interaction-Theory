I'd like to clarify a few things — not to argue, but because they matter to me.

**On "pseudo-science":** It was never my intention to produce pseudo-scientific work. I fully understand why the manuscript reads that way — it is sketchy, the parameters are not elaborated, and the scope is enormous. The reason is simple: I wrote it down first and foremost to record a line of thought — not to publish, not to seek recognition. I may have misunderstood the purpose of arXiv. I thought it was a place to timestamp ideas. If that was a misuse of the platform, I take the correction.

**What I actually want:** I am not trying to publish a polished paper. I have a single question: *Is the direction of thinking — that classical physics is the high-frequency limit of quantum mutual interaction — right or wrong?* The parameters (f², α) are not elaborated because I first wanted to know whether the overall approach makes any sense at all. The specific formula is secondary. The direction is what I wanted someone to check.

**On the process:** I believe that in learning and research, things rarely start fully formed. Sometimes you need to voice an unfinished idea to see what is wrong with it. That is all I was trying to do here.

**On ambition:** I have none. I have already settled on my path: I will continue working as an engineer, writing code and building good products to earn a living. This quantum mechanics model is something that emerged from a hobby — nothing more. You may or may not remember: I spent a year studying quantum optics, and still ended up with a 4.0. That was not because I did not study hard, or because I was not smart enough. My mind is wired in a way that it naturally refuses to absorb anything that cannot be made fully logically self-consistent from first principles. I cannot do what some other students do — memorize useful intermediate formulas and conclusions just well enough to score on an exam. My system simply rejects it. — just as I have never been someone who chases academic reputation or career success. I honestly do not understand why most people pursue these things. My life is guided by my own compass. These external metrics have never been my measure, and they never will be. (By the way, to avoid any possible misunderstanding: my email address is qin1.0@foxmail.com. The "1.0" is not a reference to the German grade 1.0 — far from it. It comes from my favorite science fiction trilogy, *The Three-Body Problem*, in which the human-computer operating system is named "Qin 1.0" — precisely my name. I loved the resonance so much that I adopted it. If you are ever interested, I would be delighted to send you and Chang the German or English edition of the trilogy. The translations, I must say, are even more beautiful than the Chinese original.) It was because, after a painful year of learning, I came to believe that the logical structure of quantum theory — as it is taught — genuinely has gaps. It does not flow from first principles as elegantly, self-consistently, or universally as Newton, Lagrange, or Maxwell. It lacks the physical intuition — the *Bild* — that those theories possess. Instead, it relies on complex mathematical derivations to force conclusions upon the reader. In short: you can compute a correct answer mathematically, but the process does not let you truly *see* what is physically happening. This makes it hard to believe that its first principles are truly free of internal contradiction. Is this observation wrong? It dodges the fundamental question of wave-particle duality with a renamed "complementarity principle," replacing a real problem with a human-made dichotomy. The Q-parameter I proposed — Q for Quantum — is precisely my attempt to find a first-principles way to compute, self-consistently and without hand-waving, how classical or how quantum a system is under given conditions. I may not succeed. But that is the goal. Is this at least the right direction to ask? This was one of the most painful intellectual experiences of my life — but I never stopped believing that there must be a beautiful, first-principles explanation underneath. Dodging the question is not the physicist's way. I have no intention whatsoever of using it to gain academic reputation, because I am simply not on that track. I don't need a title, a publication, or recognition. What I need is a way to have my thoughts examined from outside. The mere possibility of putting a draft on a platform and receiving feedback — even critical feedback — is already the purpose for me. Not publication.

**If you are interested after all:** I would be happy to prepare a more detailed version — with full step-by-step derivation of the parameters, explicit calculation of every Q-value in the table, and most importantly, a concrete experimental proposal (Bell test in low-pressure inert gas). Theory aside — an experiment that no one has ever performed is worth doing.

But if the idea as it stands does not interest you — I completely understand. I will continue to refine and test it in my own way. If there is ever a conclusion, successful or otherwise, I would be glad to share it with you. After all, failure is the mother of success.

**On AI:** You mentioned the manuscript reads like it might be AI-generated. Let me be open about this: I did use AI. I have worked with AI tools for a long time — training, refining, evolving how I use them. Given my limited free time outside work, AI helped me structure and draft the manuscript. But every sentence was reviewed, corrected, and rewritten multiple times by me. Nothing in that paper was written casually or left unchecked. If the paper has flaws — and it does — those flaws are entirely a reflection of my own current ability and my rushed timeline. Not of the tool. In my experience, AI output depends entirely on the person using it. A tool does not determine quality — the human does. AI will inevitably be used by more people in more places. I believe we should not assume that using AI inherently lowers quality. It depends on how, and by whom.

You have every right to decline the endorsement. That does not disappoint me — it is your professional assessment, and I respect it fully. I am responsible for my own questions.
